1. What is Open Channel Flow?

Open channel flow occurs when water flows with a free surface exposed to the atmosphere. Unlike pressurized pipe flow, the driving force is gravity ‚Äî the water surface slope provides the energy gradient.

Natural vs. Man-Made Channels

  • Natural channels ‚Äî rivers, streams, floodplains. Irregular cross-sections, variable roughness, and meandering alignments.
  • Man-made channels ‚Äî drainage ditches, irrigation canals, roadside swales, stormwater channels. Designed with regular geometry (trapezoidal, rectangular) for predictable hydraulic performance.

3. Flow Classification

The Froude number classifies flow behavior based on the ratio of inertial forces to gravitational forces:

Fr = V / ‚àö(g √ó Dh)    where   Dh = A / T
Froude NumberFlow RegimeCharacteristics
Fr < 1Subcritical Slow, deep, tranquil flow. Disturbances propagate upstream. Common in mild-slope channels.
Fr = 1Critical Transition point. Minimum specific energy for a given discharge. Unstable ‚Äî avoid in design.
Fr > 1Supercritical Fast, shallow, shooting flow. Disturbances cannot travel upstream. Steep slopes, spillways.

Hydraulic Jump

When supercritical flow transitions to subcritical, a hydraulic jump occurs ‚Äî a turbulent, energy-dissipating phenomenon. The water surface rises abruptly, converting kinetic energy to potential energy and heat. Hydraulic jumps are used in stilling basins downstream of dams and spillways.

5. Normal Depth & Critical Depth

Normal Depth (yn)

Normal depth is the depth at which uniform flow occurs ‚Äî the gravitational force driving the water exactly balances the friction resisting it. At normal depth, the water surface is parallel to the channel bed.

Since area A and hydraulic radius R both depend on depth y, Manning's equation cannot be solved directly for y. Instead, we solve it iteratively ‚Äî manningcalc uses a bisection method to find the depth where the computed Q matches the target Q.

Critical Depth (yc)

Critical depth is the depth where specific energy is minimum for a given discharge, or equivalently where Fr = 1. It is found by solving:

Q¬≤ √ó T / (g √ó A¬≥) = 1

This also requires iterative solution since A and T depend on y. The critical depth separates subcritical (y > yc) from supercritical (y < yc) flow.

Design rule of thumb: For stable channel design, ensure the normal depth is well above critical depth (subcritical flow) to avoid unstable flow conditions and potential hydraulic jumps.

7. Glossary

Manning's n
Empirical roughness coefficient reflecting channel surface friction. Ranges from ~0.010 (smooth concrete) to 0.060+ (dense brush floodplain). Selected from published tables based on channel material and condition.
Hydraulic Radius (R)
Ratio of flow cross-sectional area to wetted perimeter: R = A / P. Measures the channel's flow efficiency ‚Äî larger R means less friction per unit of flow area.
Wetted Perimeter (P)
The length of the channel boundary in contact with water in a cross-section. Does not include the free water surface.
Top Width (T)
Width of the water surface at the top of the flow cross-section. Used to compute hydraulic depth Dh = A / T for Froude number calculation.
Froude Number (Fr)
Dimensionless ratio of flow velocity to gravity wave speed: Fr = V / ‚àö(gDh). Determines whether flow is subcritical (Fr < 1), critical (Fr = 1), or supercritical (Fr > 1).
Normal Depth (yn)
Flow depth at which uniform flow occurs ‚Äî gravity exactly balances friction. The depth Manning's equation solves for when discharge, slope, roughness, and geometry are known.
Critical Depth (yc)
Depth at which specific energy is minimum for a given discharge (Fr = 1). Separates subcritical from supercritical flow regimes.
Specific Energy (E)
Total energy per unit weight relative to the channel bottom: E = y + V¬≤/(2g). At critical depth, specific energy is minimized.
Uniform Flow
Steady flow where depth, velocity, and cross-section remain constant along the channel. Requires a prismatic (uniform geometry) channel with constant slope.
Slope (S)
Longitudinal bed slope of the channel, expressed as a ratio (e.g., 0.001 = 1 ft drop per 1000 ft of channel length). Also called the energy gradient slope in uniform flow.
Side Slope (z)
Horizontal-to-vertical ratio of the channel banks (H:V). A side slope of z = 2 means the bank extends 2 ft horizontally for every 1 ft of depth.
